# Phase 1 Action List

## Purpose
This document defines the first playable set of player-facing actions.

The goal is to keep actions:
- concrete
- readable
- tonally strong
- and small enough to implement cleanly

Player-facing actions should feel like office sabotage and social maneuvering.
They should **not** read like abstract system commands.

---

## Phase 1 Action Set

Prototype 1 should start with **8 core action types**.

---

## 1. Reassure

### Player-facing meaning
Calm someone down, make them feel safer, reduce panic or defensiveness.

### Best used for
- anxious NPCs
- unstable allies
- preventing someone from overreacting
- stopping a target from turning hostile

### Likely effects
- lowers tension
- raises playerLikability on success
- can lower playerSuspicion if done credibly
- may reinforce bonds instead of breaking them

### Risks
- fails if used on someone who wants force, proof, or leverage instead of comfort

---

## 2. Redirect

### Player-facing meaning
Give someone a more interesting target, topic, or social path.

### Best used for
- gossip-driven NPCs
- attention-seeking NPCs
- room-level deflection

### Likely effects
- lowers office focus on the email
- creates or strengthens side conflict
- can weaponize existing rivalries
- often useful for staying under the radar

### Risks
- may create a new fire
- may count as dirty if obviously manipulative

---

## 3. Warn

### Player-facing meaning
Tell someone directly that something is coming, or that they are about to be exposed to fallout.

### Best used for
- high-status NPCs
- people who value directness
- reducing blindsiding

### Likely effects
- raises respect from directness-valuing NPCs
- may lower playerSuspicion if done early
- can reduce intervention risk with the right target

### Risks
- if done too late, may look self-protective
- if done to the wrong person, can raise awareness and confidence

---

## 4. Verify

### Player-facing meaning
Bring proof, specifics, technical facts, or firsthand confirmation.

### Best used for
- Devon-type characters
- reducing rumor ambiguity
- strengthening a controlled version of the story

### Likely effects
- raises confidence in a chosen version
- helps fact-driven allies
- may reduce chaos if the player needs a clean path

### Risks
- can also harden the email story if the facts hurt the player
- may increase office focus if proof becomes more concrete

---

## 5. Start Rumor

### Player-facing meaning
Seed a side narrative, alternative suspicion, or shiny new social problem.

### Best used for
- Betty-type characters
- splitting alliances
- redirecting room attention

### Likely effects
- raises side conflict
- can lower focus on the email
- can break or strain bonds
- useful for chaos-diversion play

### Risks
- dirty action
- can raise playerSuspicion fast if traced back
- may create future intervention conditions

---

## 6. Isolate

### Player-facing meaning
Separate a person from an ally, a source of reinforcement, or a key location.

### Best used for
- alliance disruption
- stopping rumor transfer
- breaking confidence loops

### Likely effects
- reduces sentiment transfer temporarily
- weakens alliances in key moments
- can make later persuasion attempts more viable

### Risks
- may be obvious
- may increase hostility if the target feels manipulated

---

## 7. Distract

### Player-facing meaning
Pull someone’s attention away with inconvenience, urgency, nuisance, or workplace nonsense.

### Best used for
- buying time
- preventing a conversation
- stopping an NPC from acting immediately

### Likely effects
- delays spread
- reduces awareness growth
- can create funny office sabotage beats

### Risks
- usually temporary
- can look suspicious if overused
- may create collateral resentment

---

## 8. Sabotage

### Player-facing meaning
Use a dirtier move to interfere with access, timing, credibility, or smooth communication.

### Best used for
- desperate containment
- breaking a clean chain of events
- forcing the room into disorder

### Likely effects
- can sharply reduce immediate spread
- can create chaos-diversion opportunities
- can damage key bonds

### Risks
- high dirty-play cost
- raises playerSuspicion strongly
- increases chance of intervention ending

---

## Prototype Action Rule

Every action should resolve through:
- target
- context
- hidden NPC state
- playerLikability
- playerSuspicion
- alliance effects
- current office focus

This means the same action can succeed or fail differently depending on the room.

---

## Clean vs Dirty Actions

### Usually cleaner
- Reassure
- Warn
- Verify

### Mixed
- Redirect
- Distract
- Isolate

### Usually dirty
- Start Rumor
- Sabotage

This distinction matters because dirty actions should raise suspicion faster.

---

## Important Design Rule

The action list should stay player-facing and concrete.

Good:
- reassure Tim
- distract Betty
- warn Celia
- verify with Devon
- isolate Tim from Betty

Bad:
- reduce transfer probability
- alter suspicion vector
- influence relational sentiment

Those are system descriptions, not game actions.

---

## Prototype Summary

Phase 1 actions should let the player:
- calm
- redirect
- warn
- prove
- seed conflict
- separate allies
- buy time
- get dirty

That is enough for the first playable without bloating the system.
