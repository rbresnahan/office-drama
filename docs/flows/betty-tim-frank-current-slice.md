# Betty, Tim, Frank Current Slice Flow

## Purpose

This document defines the current playable slice.

This is the slice that should be refined before adding anything new.

## Scope

The current slice includes:

- Betty as the opening conversation and intel source.
- Tim as the lactose/meeting disruption target.
- Frank as the drunk/scapegoat target.
- Kitchen as the booze discovery location.
- Closet as the backup toilet paper discovery location.
- Bathroom as the immediate toilet paper chaos location.
- All-hands as the end-of-day resolution.

## High-Level Flow

The intended current flow is:

1. Player starts the day.
2. Player talks to Betty.
3. Betty gives useful office intel.
4. Player chooses a scheme direction.
5. Player explores existing locations.
6. Player inspects specific areas.
7. Player discovers useful details or objects.
8. Player takes action.
9. The office visibly reacts.
10. Hidden fallout accumulates.
11. Schedule pressure increases.
12. All-hands resolves the player’s choices.

## Opening: Betty

Betty should remain the cleanest opening path into the game.

She should provide enough information for the player to understand possible directions without dumping the entire solution.

Betty can point toward:

- Tim’s sensitivity around food, meetings, or procedures.
- Frank’s unstable reputation.
- The kitchen as a place where office habits become visible.
- Lisa as someone who hears things quickly.
- Devin as someone who gravitates toward Tim.
- The idea that people move and talk after the player leaves.

Betty does not need to fully explain every scheme.

She should create leads.

## Scheme Route 1: Tim Lactose / Missed Meetings

### Goal

The player uses Tim’s lactose issue or meeting habits to make Tim unreliable before all-hands.

### Required Ingredients

The player needs to learn that Tim has a relevant weakness.

The player needs a way to pressure or disrupt Tim.

The player needs the office schedule to matter.

### Possible Player Actions

The player may:

- Learn about Tim from Betty.
- Push conversation toward Tim.
- Manipulate timing around meetings.
- Create food-related or anxiety-related pressure.
- Cause Tim to leave, miss, or mishandle a meeting.

### Visible Aftermath Examples

After the player acts:

- Devin walks over to Tim.
- Tim leaves his desk too quickly.
- Tim starts documenting something.
- Tim looks unsettled near a meeting time.
- Someone notices Tim is missing or late.

### Hidden Fallout Examples

Offscreen:

- Tim suspects the player.
- Devin warns Tim.
- Lisa hears that the player was asking about Tim.
- Betty tells someone too much.
- Tim starts creating a paper trail.

### All-Hands Payoff

At all-hands, this route should acknowledge whether Tim was successfully disrupted.

Possible outcomes:

- Tim’s credibility is damaged.
- Tim misses key context.
- Tim appears unreliable.
- Tim exposes the player’s suspicious behavior.
- Tim survives the scheme and becomes dangerous.

## Scheme Route 2: Frank Drunk / Scapegoat

### Goal

The player uses Frank’s reputation and the discovered bottle to make Frank look like the office liability.

### Required Ingredients

The player needs to discover the bottle through kitchen inspection.

The player needs to understand Frank is a plausible scapegoat.

The player needs to connect Frank to the bottle or the idea of drinking.

### Kitchen Discovery Flow

Initial kitchen visit:

The bottle should not appear as a raw choice.

The player should inspect the fridge or top of the fridge.

After inspection, the player discovers the bottle.

Then the player can choose to take it or leave it.

Taking the bottle should create state changes.

### Possible Player Actions

The player may:

- Take the bottle.
- Leave the bottle as observed evidence.
- Use the bottle to imply Frank is drinking.
- Push conversation toward Frank’s reputation.
- Let someone else notice the bottle is missing.
- Create enough noise that Frank reacts badly.

### Visible Aftermath Examples

After the player acts:

- Celia glances at the player’s bag after leaving the kitchen.
- Frank reacts loudly.
- Lisa watches Frank.
- Someone looks toward the kitchen.
- Frank becomes defensive.

### Hidden Fallout Examples

Offscreen:

- Celia suspects the player took something.
- Lisa hears the bottle is missing.
- Someone connects Frank to the kitchen.
- Frank suspects he is being set up.
- Betty repeats something that complicates the story.

### All-Hands Payoff

At all-hands, this route should acknowledge whether Frank became the plausible problem.

Possible outcomes:

- Frank absorbs blame.
- Frank gets angry and makes things worse.
- Lisa does not fully buy it.
- Celia noticed enough to undercut the player.
- The player survives because Frank looks worse.

## Scheme Route 3: Pure Chaos

### Goal

The player creates enough office disruption that the original problem gets buried under noise.

This route uses the current environment rather than adding a new major scheme.

### Required Ingredients

The player needs discoverable environmental objects.

The player needs actions that create small but escalating disruptions.

The player needs visible office reactions.

### Closet Flow

Initial closet visit:

The player inspects the shelves.

The player discovers backup toilet paper.

The player can take one roll, take all rolls, or leave it.

Taking one roll should be lower risk.

Taking all rolls should be higher risk.

### Bathroom Flow

Initial bathroom visit:

The player inspects the stall.

The player discovers the toilet paper situation is fragile.

The player can remove the roll or leave it.

Removing the bathroom roll should be higher risk because it will be discovered quickly.

### Possible Player Actions

The player may:

- Remove toilet paper from the closet.
- Remove toilet paper from the bathroom.
- Take the bottle.
- Create timing-based disruptions.
- Cause office movement.
- Make people interrupt Lisa, Tim, Frank, or Betty.

### Visible Aftermath Examples

After the player acts:

- Someone heads toward the bathroom.
- Someone returns from the bathroom visibly irritated.
- Lisa gets interrupted.
- Frank complains.
- Tim loses focus.
- Devin checks on Tim.
- Betty watches the office movement.

### Hidden Fallout Examples

Offscreen:

- Lisa connects multiple disruptions.
- Tim starts documenting.
- Celia notices timing.
- Betty becomes nervous about helping.
- Devin tells Tim something.

### All-Hands Payoff

At all-hands, this route should acknowledge whether chaos became the dominant office problem.

Possible outcomes:

- The office is too distracted to focus clearly.
- The chaos backfires and points toward the player.
- Lisa cuts through the noise.
- Frank or Tim becomes the loudest problem.
- The player survives because everyone else is busy fighting.

## Shared Consequence Layer

All routes should use the same consequence model.

After meaningful choices:

- Apply immediate result text.
- Apply state changes.
- Queue visible aftermath when appropriate.
- Add hidden fallout when appropriate.
- Advance time when appropriate.
- Let later scenes reference what happened.

## Important Design Rule

Visible aftermath should show what the player can observe.

Hidden fallout should store what the player does not directly know.

Example:

Visible:

Betty walks toward Lisa after the player leaves.

Hidden:

Betty told Lisa the player was asking about Tim.

Later payoff:

Lisa says the player has been asking a lot about Tim.

## Minimum Complete Slice

The prototype feels complete when each current route has:

- A clear starting lead.
- At least one inspect/discovery moment.
- At least one meaningful action.
- At least one visible aftermath beat.
- At least one hidden fallout consequence.
- At least one all-hands acknowledgment.

## Final Scope Reminder

Do not expand beyond this slice yet.

Refine the current loop until it plays cleanly from start to finish.
